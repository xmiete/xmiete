> Research date: 2026-05-09. Sources: GOV.UK, EUI, Rentumo, Landager, RTB, GlobalPropertyGuide, CMS Expert Guide CEE, and others listed at the end.

## UK Detail: The Three Approved Schemes

England and Wales operate the most developed mandatory deposit protection system in Europe:

- **Deposit Protection Service (DPS)**: Government-authorized; offers both custodial (free) and insured models.
- **Tenancy Deposit Scheme (TDS)**: The only scheme that can protect commercial landlords; free ADR.
- **MyDeposits**: Insurance-based and custodial; part of the National Landlord Association group.

All three require deposit protection within 30 days of receipt and provide a free ADR service with a 28-day resolution target. Failure results in a 1–3x deposit penalty. Scotland operates custodial-only schemes and caps deposits at 2 months.

---

## Switzerland: The Blocked Account Mandate

Switzerland has the strictest escrow rule without a government scheme: deposits must be held in a blocked bank account (Mietkautionskonto) in the **tenant's name** (Art. 257e CO). The landlord cannot access the funds — they are released by the bank only on mutual agreement or court order. The 3-month cap is absolute. An insurance alternative market (SwissCaution, goCaution, FirstCaution) has emerged, costing 4–6% of deposit per year.

---

## France: ALUR Law Mechanics

The ALUR law (2014) created a two-tier return system: 1 month if the exit inventory matches entry; 2 months if any difference is noted. The 10% per month (of rent) penalty for late returns is the strongest financial penalty for landlord delay in Europe alongside the UK's 1–3x penalty.

---

## Spain: The Fianza System

Spain is unique in mandating deposit registration with regional public bodies within 30 days. The legal deposit (fianza = 1 month) is held by the state authority; any additional guarantee (up to 2 extra months) stays with the landlord. Andalucía abolished this requirement in 2025, showing the system is fragmenting by region.

---

## EU Supranational Harmonization

**There is no EU directive on residential rental deposits.** Residential tenancy law is entirely national. The European University Institute has noted harmonization is "not only politically unrealistic, but legally excluded" from EU competences. The EU's only 2024 intervention is an STR registration regulation — it does not touch residential deposits.

---

## What Is Truly Common Across Europe

1. **Security deposit is universal practice** — even where not legally mandated (Romania, Bulgaria, Serbia), it is standard in urban markets.
2. **3-month cap is the dominant model** — Germany, Switzerland, Austria, Czech Republic, Denmark, Finland, Estonia, Hungary, Italy, Spain (total), Sweden, Norway (statutory max). France, Malta, Cyprus, Ireland are the outliers at 1–2 months.
3. **Normal wear and tear cannot be deducted** — present in virtually every legal system.
4. **Written documentation matters everywhere** — entry/exit inventories are legally required or strongly recommended in all jurisdictions.
5. **Interest on deposit belongs to tenant** — codified in Germany, Austria, Czech Republic, Hungary, Italy; general principle in most others.
6. **Landlord must itemize and justify deductions** — no jurisdiction allows arbitrary retention.

## What Varies Significantly

| Dimension | Range |
|---|---|
| **Maximum deposit** | 1 month (Malta, Cyprus, Ireland, France/unfurnished) → 2 months (Belgium, Luxembourg, France/furnished, Netherlands, Portugal) → 3 months (most of EU) → 6 months (Norway statutory max; Denmark total move-in cost) → No cap (Bulgaria, Croatia, Slovenia, Serbia, Greece) |
| **Government protection scheme** | Mandatory scheme (UK, Belgium) → Mandatory separate/blocked account (Germany, Switzerland, Norway, Austria, Malta) → Regional authority registration (Spain) → No requirement (France, Netherlands, most CEE, Southern Europe) |
| **Return deadline** | 10 days (Latvia) → 2–3 weeks (Norway, Malta) → 1 month (France clean, Spain, Czech Republic, Portugal) → 2 months (France disputed, Italy, Estonia) → 3–6 months (Germany practice) → No statutory deadline (Ireland, Sweden, Finland, Romania, Bulgaria, Greece) |
| **Dispute resolution** | Specialist tribunal (UK, Norway, Denmark, Sweden, Netherlands, Ireland RTB) → Consumer board (Finland) → General civil courts only (most Southern and Eastern Europe) |
| **Escrow/account requirement** | Blocked account in tenant's name (Switzerland, Norway) → Separate landlord account required (Germany, Austria, Belgium, Malta) → Regional authority holds deposit (Spain fianza) → Landlord holds freely (France, Netherlands, Italy, most CEE) |
| **Interest obligation** | Required (Germany, Austria, Czech Republic, Italy, Hungary) → Not required (France, Netherlands, Spain, Ireland, most CEE) |

## Key Divergences a Pan-European Standard Must Account For

1. **Deposit cap per country**: A single "3-month default" works for most markets but must flex — France caps at 1–2 months, Malta/Cyprus/Ireland at 1 month.
2. **Account structure is country-specific**: Switzerland requires a blocked account in the tenant's name (no landlord-held model is legal). Germany/Austria/Belgium require segregated accounts. France and most CEE allow landlord-held deposits.
3. **Spain's fianza registration workflow**: Regional authority registration is required within 30 days — a mandatory workflow that no other country has (and Andalucía abolished it in 2025, adding regional fragmentation).
4. **Return timer cannot be unified**: Valid deadlines range from 10 days (Latvia) to 6 months (Germany in practice) — release logic needs a per-country timer.
5. **Interest calculation**: Required in Germany, Austria, Czech Republic, Italy, Hungary — not required elsewhere.
6. **Dispute routing**: UK, Norway, Denmark, Sweden, Netherlands, Ireland have specialist ADR bodies with structured processes; Southern and Eastern Europe route disputes to general civil courts.
7. **Ireland's pending reform**: The 2025 Deposits Bill could make Ireland require RTB-held deposits — build to accommodate this transition.
8. **CEE legal fragility**: Romania, Bulgaria, Serbia have no statutory framework — the platform must rely entirely on contractual terms with no regulatory backstop.
9. **Scotland vs. England**: Even within the UK, Scotland caps at 2 months and requires custodial-only protection.
10. **Currency**: UK (GBP), Switzerland (CHF), Norway (NOK), Sweden (SEK), Denmark (DKK), Poland (PLN), Czech Republic (CZK), Hungary (HUF) are non-EUR.
